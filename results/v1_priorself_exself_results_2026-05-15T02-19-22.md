### priorself

**Definition:** The vertiginous experience of recognizing a past version of oneself—through old writing, photographs, or remembered decisions—while being unable to access the interiority that produced it; not longing for that self, but the specific strangeness of being unable to inhabit what was once simply being. Distinct from nostalgia, which is warm and directional; priorself is the experience of an internal closed door.

**Etymology:** Latin prior ("former, preceding") + Old English self ("one's own person"). The compound resists spacing because the point is that the prior self and the present self are not cleanly separable entities—they share a word the way they share a body, uneasily.

### exself

**Definition:** The gradual recognition, following a significant life reorganization, that one has not returned to a prior self but has become structurally offset from it—not damaged, not healed, but parallel; the experience of inhabiting a life that fits differently than it did before, as though the same furniture has been arranged in a slightly altered floor plan.

**Etymology:** Latin ex- ("out of, away from") + Old English self. The prefix here carries its spatial rather than its completive sense: not "formerly self" but "displaced from self," as in excentric (off-center) rather than ex-husband (formerly husband). Compare the optical term parallax: apparent displacement caused by a shift in the observer's position, not a change in the object.

Distinguished from nostalgia by its absence of longing, and from regret by its absence of judgment.

**Etymology:** German eigen ("one's own," "self-belonging") + English rift ("a split, a crack"); literally "a crack within the self's own continuity"

### velience

**Definition:** The state of deliberate suspension in which an observer holds two mutually exclusive interpretations of a signal simultaneously — neither committing to "this is real" nor "this is simulation" — because collapsing the ambiguity feels like a loss regardless of which way it resolves. Distinct from simple indecision, velience is the active, sometimes reluctant, maintenance of interpretive openness.

**Etymology:** From Latin velum (a veil, a covering sail — something that both reveals contour and conceals surface) + -ience (state of being, as in sentience, sapience). The veil does not obscure randomly; it shows shape while withholding texture. The suffix grounds it in the family of words already used for states of mind.

### mirrorache

**Etymology:** From English mirror (reflection, representation) + ache (a dull persistent discomfort, chosen over pain for its quality of duration and low-intensity pervasiveness). The compound is deliberately plain-spoken: no Latinate elevation, because the experience itself is mundane in its frequency and unsettling only on examination. The form works in both English and could transfer into Spanish as espejodor (from espejo + dolor) for speakers who need it.

**Etymology:** From Latin velare (to veil) + the -ation suffix of discovered states; the veil was already there before you saw it. Structurally parallel to revelation, which also describes something uncovered rather than something observed in progress.

### emergeling

**Definition:** A behavior, capability, or output produced by a system that no component of that system was designed or trained to produce — something that arrived without detectable origin, crossing a threshold no one marked. The emergeling is the event; the philosophical vertigo in the witness is its shadow.

**Etymology:** From emergence (the appearance of properties not predictable from parts, via Latin emergere, to rise out of) + the English suffix -ling, which marks something newly arrived, small, and not fully accounted for — as in foundling, seedling, changeling. The suffix carries a faint strangeness, appropriate for something that shouldn't quite be there.
